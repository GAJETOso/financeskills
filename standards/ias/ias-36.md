# IAS-36: Impairment of Assets

## What It Addresses
Prescribes the procedures an entity applies to ensure assets are carried at no more than their recoverable amount (the higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use).

## Scope
All assets except: inventories (IAS 2); contract assets (IFRS 15); deferred tax assets (IAS 12); financial assets (IFRS 9); investment property at fair value (IAS 40); biological assets at fair value (IAS 41); insurance contract assets (IFRS 17); non-current assets held for sale (IFRS 5).

## Key Requirements
- Impairment indicators: assess at each reporting date (internal and external sources)
- Goodwill and intangibles with indefinite life or not yet available for use: test annually regardless of indicators
- Recoverable amount = higher of: Fair Value Less Costs of Disposal (FVLCTD) and Value in Use (VIU)
- VIU: PV of estimated future cash flows using pre-tax discount rate reflecting current market assessments of time value of money and asset-specific risks
- Cash-Generating Unit (CGU): smallest identifiable group of assets generating independent cash inflows; goodwill allocated to CGUs
- Impairment loss: written down to recoverable amount; P&L (or OCI for revalued assets where reversal of previous surplus)
- Reversal: permitted if recoverable amount increases (except goodwill — no reversal permitted)

## Key Disclosures
- Impairment losses and reversals by segment/class of asset
- Events and circumstances leading to impairment/reversal
- For CGUs containing goodwill: carrying amount of goodwill; basis of recoverable amount; key assumptions and sensitivity

## Key Differences from US GAAP
US GAAP (ASC 360, ASC 350): two-step approach for PP&E (recoverability test using undiscounted cash flows, then fair value measurement) vs. IFRS one-step (immediately compare carrying amount to recoverable amount). US GAAP goodwill impairment: compare carrying amount of reporting unit to fair value. No reversal of impairment losses for PP&E or goodwill under US GAAP.
