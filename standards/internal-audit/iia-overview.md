# IIA Global Internal Audit Standards (GIAS) 2024

## What It Addresses
The most significant revision to IIA standards in decades. The GIAS replaces the legacy International Professional Practices Framework (IPPF) and its three-tier structure (Core Principles, Code of Ethics, Standards). The new framework is principles-based and organised into five domains.

## Why the Change?
- IPPF was compliance-focused and procedural
- GIAS is risk-based and outcomes-focused
- Explicit independence requirements for Chief Audit Executives (CAEs)
- Stronger board/audit committee accountability
- Explicit coverage of ESG, technology risk, and third-party risk

## Five Domains
| Domain | Focus |
|--------|-------|
| I | Purpose of Internal Auditing |
| II | Ethics and Professionalism |
| III | Governing the Internal Audit Function |
| IV | Managing the Internal Audit Function |
| V | Performing Internal Audit Services |

## Mandatory Elements vs. Strongly Recommended
- **Mandatory**: Domains I–V core standards — must comply or explain
- **Topical Requirements**: Additional requirements for specific topics (ESG, IT, third-party risk)
- **Guidance**: Non-mandatory; practical application support

## Key Themes
- **Independence**: CAE must report functionally to the board/audit committee; administrative reporting to CEO acceptable
- **Objectivity**: Auditors must be free from bias; cooling-off periods for those moving from operations to audit
- **Quality**: Annual quality assessment (internal); external quality assessment every 5 years
- **Stakeholder communication**: CAE must communicate audit plan, resource needs, and significant risks to the board

## Nigeria Context
- ICAN and CIBN members in internal audit roles expected to comply with IIA/GIAS
- CBN, SEC Nigeria, and NAICOM all reference IIA standards in their corporate governance frameworks
- NITDA (IT) and IPPF/GIAS increasingly referenced for public sector internal audit
