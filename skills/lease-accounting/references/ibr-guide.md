# Incremental Borrowing Rate (IBR) Determination

## Definition
The rate the lessee would pay to borrow, over a similar term, with similar security, the amount needed to obtain an asset of similar value in a similar economic environment. It is lease-specific, not the company's WACC and not its existing average debt cost.

## Build-Up Approach (the auditable method)
`IBR = Reference rate (currency + term matched) + Credit spread (entity, secured basis) + Asset/security adjustment`

1. **Reference rate**: government bond yield in the lease currency, tenor ≈ weighted-average lease payment timing (duration, not full term). NGN leases → FGN bond curve; USD → Treasury curve.
2. **Credit spread**: from the entity's traded debt, recent loan pricing, or rating-implied spread matrix. Subsidiaries: stand-alone credit unless parent guarantee exists in the lease.
3. **Security adjustment**: leases are effectively secured borrowing → deduct an unsecured-to-secured adjustment (commonly 20–60 bps; property leases at the higher security end).

## Portfolio Practicalities
- Build an IBR matrix: currency × term bucket (1–3y, 3–5y, 5–10y, 10y+) × entity, refreshed quarterly; apply to new leases by bucket.
- Document the date of each rate — IBR is set at commencement (or modification), not reporting date.
- Materiality shortcut: portfolio-level rate permitted for similar leases (IFRS 16.B1).

## Common Audit Findings
1. Using WACC or a flat round number across all leases.
2. Term mismatch (10-year lease at a 1-year rate).
3. Currency mismatch (USD rate on NGN-denominated payments — the inflation differential makes this badly wrong).
4. Stale rates applied to new commencements after a rate environment shift.
5. No security adjustment documentation.

## Sensitivity
Disclose/monitor: ±100 bps typically moves the liability ~4–8% on a 10-year lease. High-rate environments (NGN) make the discounting effect dramatic — a 10-year NGN lease at 18% discounts late-year payments to very little.
